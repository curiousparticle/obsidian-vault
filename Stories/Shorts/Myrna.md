UPADATE!

  

**Act 1 — Setup (~25%, ~2,000 words)**

1.  **Hook / Opening Image** (~600w). Drop us into the protagonist's normal world, but start _close_ to the trouble. Short fiction can't afford five pages of throat-clearing — establish character, tone, and the world's texture fast, ideally with the inciting incident already casting its shadow.
2.  **Inciting Incident** (~700w). The disruption that knocks the status quo sideways. In a short story this lands early — usually inside the first 20% — because you don't have the runway a novel does.
3.  **The Lock-In** (~700w). The protagonist commits (or is forced) to a course of action. The door closes behind them. This is what ends Act 1 — the point of no return.

**Act 2 — Confrontation (~50%, ~4,000 words)**

1.  **Rising Action / Progressive Complications** (~1,200w). Attempts to solve the problem, each obstacle escalating. The rule that keeps Act 2 from sagging: every scene must _turn_ — end in a different emotional or situational place than it began. Each attempt should cost something or reveal something.
2.  **Midpoint Turn** (~900w). A reversal, revelation, or stakes-raise that changes what the protagonist _understands_. Often a false victory or false defeat. This is the spine of the whole story — if it's flabby, everything droops.
3.  **The Squeeze** (~1,100w). Pressure intensifies, plans fail, the cost rises. The screws tighten specifically against the protagonist's flaw or blind spot.
4.  **Crisis / All Is Lost** (~800w). The darkest moment, where the dilemma sharpens to a single unavoidable point and the protagonist must confront the _real_ (usually internal) issue.

**Act 3 — Resolution (~25%, ~2,000 words)**

1.  **Climax** (~1,200w). The decisive choice or confrontation. The protagonist acts on what they've learned — and the question the story posed gets answered. Highest tension lives here.
2.  **Resolution / Final Image** (~800w). The new normal. Mirror the opening image to show the transformation (or its poignant absence). This is the Ghibli exhale — the quiet beat after the storm where the kettle goes back on and you see what's actually changed.
