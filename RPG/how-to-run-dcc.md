# How to Run DCC   
Running a great session of **Dungeon Crawl Classics (DCC)**—a game known for its old-school feel, lethal challenges, and creative problem-solving—requires a mix of preparation, improvisation, and a focus on player engagement. Here are some key strategies to keep your players hooked and make your sessions memorable:   
 --- 
### 1. Embrace the DCC Mindset   
- **Lethality & Consequences:** DCC is known for its high stakes. Players should feel that their choices matter, and failure can be punishing. This creates tension and excitement, but always ensure that failure is fun and leads to new opportunities.   
- **Player Creativity:** Reward creative solutions, not just dice rolls. If a player suggests a wild plan, let them try it—even if it’s not in the rules. This encourages engagement and investment in the world.   
 --- 
   
### 2. Session Structure & Pacing   
- **Start Strong:** Open with a vivid scene or immediate action—ambush, mysterious discovery, or a pressing question—to grab attention.   
- **Keep It Moving:** DCC thrives on momentum. Avoid getting bogged down in rules or lengthy descriptions. Use timers for in-game events (e.g., “The torch burns for 10 more minutes”) to create urgency.   
- **Cliffhangers:** End sessions on a tense note—a revelation, a trap sprung, or a looming threat—to keep players eager for the next game.   
 --- 
   
### 3. Player Engagement Techniques   
- **Ask Questions:** Involve players in world-building. Ask, *“What does your character know about this cult?”* or *“How did you hear about this dungeon?”* This makes the world feel collaborative.   
- **Spotlight Rotation:** Ensure every player gets a moment to shine, especially in large groups. Tailor challenges to different characters’ strengths.   
- **In-Character Interaction:** Encourage roleplay by addressing players as their characters and rewarding in-character dialogue.   
 --- 
   
### 4. Dungeon Design & Challenges   
- **Non-Combat Challenges:** Use puzzles, traps, and environmental hazards to break up combat. DCC’s spell system and weird magic items are perfect for creative problem-solving.   
- **Random Tables:** Lean into DCC’s love of randomness. Use tables for dungeon dressing, NPC reactions, and treasure to keep things unpredictable.   
- **Factions & Rivalries:** Populate your world with factions (cults, guilds, monsters) that players can ally with, betray, or manipulate. This adds depth and replayability.   
 --- 
   
### 5. Reward Exploration & Risk-Taking   
- **Treasure as Motivation:** DCC’s magic items are often bizarre and powerful. Dangle unique rewards to encourage players to take risks.   
- **Fame & Infamy:** Track the party’s reputation. Are they feared, respected, or hunted? This gives players a sense of impact on the world.   
- **Session Recap:** Start each session with a quick recap, highlighting player achievements. This reinforces their agency and builds excitement.   
 --- 
   
### 6. Handling Combat   
- **Dynamic Battles:** Use terrain, environmental hazards, and morale rules to make combat tactical and unpredictable.   
- **Narrative Damage:** Describe injuries and near-misses vividly. A “1 HP left” moment should feel dramatic, not just mechanical.   
- **Retreat as an Option:** Not every fight should be to the death. Sometimes, running away is the smartest (and most fun) choice.   
 --- 
   
### 7. Post-Session Feedback   
- **Quick Debrief:** Ask players what they enjoyed and what could be improved. Adjust your style based on their feedback.   
- **Session Zero:** If starting a new campaign, discuss expectations, tone, and house rules upfront to align everyone’s vision.   
 --- 
   
### 8. Resources & Inspiration   
- **Modules & Adventures:** DCC’s published adventures (like *Sailors on the Starless Sea* or *The People of the Pit*) are excellent for inspiration.   
- **Community Tips:** Check out forums, blogs, and actual-play podcasts (e.g., *Spellburn*) for ideas and advice from experienced judges.   
 --- 
   
**Final Thought:**
DCC shines when the judge (you) creates a living, reactive world and lets the players’ choices drive the story—even if those choices lead to disaster. The goal is to make every session feel like a legendary tale, whether it ends in triumph or a TPK (Total Party Kill).
