# Normalizing the Cleric   
Great observation! In **Dungeon Crawl Classics (DCC)** and many OSR games, clerics often become the "healing bottleneck," which can create an over-reliance on their magic and make them feel like the only indispensable class. The good news is that you can **reduce the cleric’s dominance** while keeping the game dangerous and engaging. Here’s how:   
 --- 
### 1. Decentralize Healing   
**Goal:** Make healing a group effort, not just the cleric’s job.   
### Alternative Healing Sources   
- **Herbalism & Folk Remedies:** Introduce healing herbs, poultices, or potions that non-clerics can find, craft, or buy. These could be rare or require downtime to prepare, but they provide a non-magical way to recover HP.   
    - *Example:* A "Woundwort" herb might heal 1d4 HP but requires a successful skill check to prepare.   
- **Downtime Recovery:** Allow characters to recover HP slowly during rest (e.g., 1 HP per day of downtime in a safe location). This encourages strategic planning and resource management.   
- **Patron-Blessed Items:** Non-clerics could use holy symbols, relics, or blessed water (limited uses) to stabilize dying characters or heal minor wounds.   
   
### Class Abilities   
- **Lay on Hands for Warriors:** Give warriors or other classes a limited ability to stabilize dying allies (e.g., once per session, no HP recovery, just stopping bleeding).   
- **Thief’s "Lucky Escape":** Thieves could have a chance to "cheat death" once per session, automatically stabilizing at 1 HP if they drop to 0.   
 --- 
   
### 2. Nerf Cleric Healing (Without Removing It)   
**Goal:** Make cleric healing less reliable or more costly.   
### Limit Healing Spells   
- **Reduced Healing Output:** Halve the HP recovered from cleric spells (e.g., *Cure Light Wounds* heals 1d4+1 instead of 1d8+1).   
- **Risky Healing:** Healing spells could require a spell check. Failure might result in no healing, backlash damage, or attracting unwanted attention (e.g., "The healing light alerts nearby monsters").   
- **Divine Favor:** Clerics must roll on a "Divine Favor" table when casting healing spells. Results could include:   
    - Full healing (rare).   
    - Partial healing (common).   
    - The cleric takes damage (the gods demand sacrifice).   
    - The target is healed but gains a temporary curse (e.g., "Marked by the Gods: –1 to all rolls for 1 hour").   
   
### Add Costs to Healing   
- **Material Components:** Healing spells might require rare or expensive components (e.g., crushed pearls, holy water, or a sacrifice of gold).   
- **Exhaustion:** Casting healing spells could impose fatigue or require the cleric to rest afterward.   
 --- 
   
### 3. Encourage Non-Healing Cleric Roles   
**Goal:** Shift the cleric’s focus away from being a "healbot."   
### Buff Other Cleric Abilities   
- **Divine Protection:** Enhance cleric abilities like turning undead, blessing weapons, or providing temporary bonuses to allies (e.g., +1 to saves vs. poison).   
- **Utility Spells:** Give clerics more non-healing spells (e.g., *Create Water*, *Purify Food*, *Divination*) to make them versatile support characters.   
- **Rituals & Downtime:** Clerics could perform rituals to remove curses, bless campsites, or gain temporary boons for the party.   
   
### Thematic Restrictions   
- **Deity-Specific Rules:** Tie healing to the cleric’s deity. For example:   
    - A war god’s cleric might only heal warriors.   
    - A trickster god’s cleric might heal randomly (roll 1d4: 1=cleric, 2=ally, 3=enemy, 4=no one).   
    - A nature god’s cleric might only heal in natural settings (not dungeons).   
 --- 
   
### 4. Increase Lethality (But Fairly)   
**Goal:** Keep the game dangerous, but ensure death isn’t the only consequence.   
### Injury Systems   
- Replace instant death with **injury tables** (as discussed earlier). This reduces the pressure on clerics to "save" characters and makes survival feel earned.   
- *Example:* A character at 0 HP rolls on an injury table. Results could include broken bones, concussions, or temporary blindness—all of which require creative solutions, not just healing spells.   
   
### Group Morale & Retreat   
- Encourage players to **retreat or adapt** when things go wrong. Make it clear that not every fight is winnable, and running away is a valid (and smart) tactic.   
- Use morale rules for enemies: If the party is struggling, enemies might flee or surrender, giving players a chance to regroup.   
 --- 
   
### 5. Rebalance the Party Dynamic   
**Goal:** Ensure no single class is indispensable.   
### Encourage Teamwork   
- **Shared Resources:** Make healing potions, bandages, or herbs available to all classes, but limit their supply. Players must decide how to allocate them.   
- **Skill Challenges:** Replace some combat encounters with skill-based challenges (e.g., escaping a trap, negotiating with a faction) where clerics aren’t the only useful class.   
   
### Class Synergy   
- **Warrior Bodyguards:** Warriors could take hits for allies or provide defensive bonuses.   
- **Thief Scouting:** Thieves could spot traps or hidden dangers, reducing the need for healing.   
- **Magic-User Utility:** Magic-users could have spells that indirectly aid survival (e.g., *Sleep* to avoid combat, *Charm* to turn enemies into allies).   
 --- 
   
### 6. Playtest & Adjust   
- **Session Zero:** Discuss the changes with your players. Explain that you’re aiming for a more balanced, teamwork-focused game.   
- **Iterate:** After a few sessions, ask for feedback. Are clerics still overpowered? Are injuries too harsh? Adjust as needed.   
 --- 
   
### Example House Rules for DCC   
1. **Cleric Healing:**   
    - *Cure Light Wounds* heals 1d4 HP (instead of 1d8) and requires a DC 12 spell check. Failure means the spell is lost, and the cleric takes 1d4 damage.   
    - Clerics can only cast healing spells **once per character per day** (e.g., no spamming *Cure Light Wounds* on the same fighter repeatedly).   
2. **Alternative Healing:**   
    - **Herbalism:** A successful Intelligence or Luck check lets a character prepare a poultice that heals 1 HP after 1 hour of rest.   
    - **Downtime:** Characters recover 1 HP per day of rest in a safe location (e.g., a town or camp).

### Why This Works   
- **Clerics are still useful** but not the sole source of healing.   
- **Players must rely on teamwork, creativity, and resource management** to survive.   
- **Injuries and consequences** keep the game dangerous and exciting, even if death is less common.   
 --- 
   
**Final Thought:**
The key is to make healing **a shared responsibility** and ensure that clerics have other ways to contribute. This keeps the game balanced, dangerous, and fun for everyone.
