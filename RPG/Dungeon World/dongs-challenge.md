# Dongs Challenge   
### Revised Dong’s Challenge: The Truth of the Fall   
 --- 
### Dong’s Vision in the Gates: The Conspiracy of Care   
As Dong presses his palm to the gate, the runes flare, and the world dissolves. He is no longer in the Iron Citadel—he is back in the **Caverns of the Moon Clan**, the night he believed he was betrayed.   
 --- 
### The Memory Unfolds   
The cavern is alive with the roar of the clan, the air thick with the scent of smoke and sweat. Dong stands at the center, his voice booming as he rallies the warriors for another reckless raid. His friends—**Zambroni, Grok, and Krewel**—exchange glances, their faces tight with concern. Dong doesn’t see it. He never does.   
- **The Incident:** The vision shifts to the tavern in **Klausterhorn**. Dong is drunk, his laughter loud and brash. Grok watches him, not with envy, but with **fear**. Dong forces Grok to drink, beer after beer, his voice slurring as he mocks Grok’s hesitation. *"You’re weak, brother!"* Dong laughs, not seeing the way Grok’s hands shake—not from fear of the ale, but from fear of *him*. The night spirals. Dong follows the women Zambroni procured, his actions reckless, his judgment clouded. The next morning, he wakes in the bowels of a ship, naked and alone.   
- **The Truth:** The gate’s runes sear the truth into Dong’s mind. The vision shifts again—this time, to a **secret meeting** in the caverns. Zambroni, Grok, and Krewel huddle together, their voices low and urgent. *"He’s going to get us all killed,"* Zambroni says. *"He’s too reckless. He doesn’t listen."* Grok’s voice is heavy with regret: *"We have to stop him. For the village."* The elders agree. They don’t exile Dong out of malice—they do it to **save the clan**.   
- **The Revelation:** The runes burn with the words: *"You were never the victim. You were the storm. And they sheltered the village from you."*   
 --- 
   
### The Challenge   
To open the gate, Dong must **acknowledge the truth**: that his friends didn’t betray him—they *protected* their people from his recklessness. The runes demand humility. Dong must kneel before the gate and speak the words aloud:   
*"I was dangerous. I put my pride before my people. And they did what they had to do."*   
 --- 
 --- 
### Dong’s Special Challenge: The Burden of Leadership   
### Mechanics: Facing the Consequences   
- If Dong admits his recklessness, the gate creaks open—but the citadel *remembers*. As the party enters, Dong is pulled into a **vision of his past self**, standing before the clan elders, drunk and defiant. The elders are not angry—they are **sad**. *"You could have been great,"* one says. *"But you let your pride blind you."*   
- **The Trial:** Dong must navigate a series of challenges that force him to confront the consequences of his actions:   
    1. **The Clan’s Judgment:** Dong is transported into the vision, invisible to all but the elders. They recount the raids he led that ended in disaster, the lives lost because of his recklessness. Dong must **listen**—truly listen—to their words, without interruption or excuse.   
        - **Defy Danger (WIS):** Roll +WIS to swallow his pride and accept their judgment.   
            - **10+:** Dong gains insight into the true weight of leadership. The elders nod, their expressions softening. *"Leadership isn’t about strength. It’s about wisdom."* The vision shifts, and Dong rejoins the party—but the citadel’s guardians now test his humility, forcing him to rely on his allies to survive.   
            - **7-9:** Dong struggles to accept their words. The vision lingers, and the citadel’s corridors shift to reflect his inner turmoil. He must perform an act of humility before the end of the session, or the citadel will turn against him.   
            - **Miss:** Dong’s pride gets the better of him. He lashes out, and the elders’ sadness turns to anger. The vision becomes a nightmare, and Dong must **fight his way out**—but every strike against the elders’ spectral forms deals damage to his own spirit (lose 1d4 CHA permanently).   
    2. **The Offer:** The vision shifts again. Grok appears, no longer the jealous rival Dong believed him to be, but a weary leader. *"You could have been great,"* Grok says. *"But you weren’t ready. Maybe you still aren’t."* He offers Dong a choice:   
        - **Accept the truth and kneel before him**, acknowledging that Grok acted for the good of the clan (lose 1d4 CHA, but the vision ends, and Dong rejoins the party with a newfound sense of humility).   
        - **Refuse and face the consequences** (the citadel’s guardians now target Dong’s pride, forcing him to rely on his allies to survive).   
    3. **The Lesson:** If Dong accepts the truth, the vision shifts one final time. He sees the clan—not as they were when he left, but as they could be, thriving under Grok’s leadership. The villagers are happy, safe. *"This is what leadership looks like,"* a voice whispers. Dong realizes that Grok’s rise wasn’t built on betrayal—it was built on **responsibility**.   
 --- 
   
### Twist: The Gem of Truth-Seeing   
- If Dong succeeds in his trial, the elders reveal a secret: *"The Gem of Truth-Seeing is hidden in this citadel. Take it, and you’ll see the truth of your past—and the truth of your future."* This sets up a **moral choice** for Dong: Does he take the gem to finally understand the depth of his recklessness, knowing it may force him to return to Great Mud and face his past? Or does he leave it, and let his old self remain a cautionary tale?   
 --- 
 --- 
   
### Why This Works for Dong’s Arc   
- **Emotional Growth:** Dong’s challenge forces him to confront his own flaws—not as a victim, but as someone who *needed* to be stopped. This is a pivotal moment for his character, especially given his recent role as a leader among the party.   
- **Consequences:** The trial doesn’t just open a gate—it changes how the citadel interacts with Dong, reinforcing the theme that leadership requires humility and wisdom.   
- **Future Hooks:** The Gem of Truth-Seeing ties directly into Dong’s ongoing story, giving you a powerful tool to challenge him (and the party) later in the campaign.
