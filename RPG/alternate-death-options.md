# Alternate Death Options   
### 1. Replace Death with Lasting Consequences   
Instead of killing characters outright, introduce **injuries, scars, or long-term effects** that make survival feel meaningful and heroic.   
### Injury Tables   
- **Critical Hits:** When a character drops to 0 HP, roll on an injury table (e.g., broken bones, lost eyes, permanent scars) instead of instant death.   
- **Recovery Costs:** Injuries could require downtime, gold, or quests to heal, adding depth to the world and encouraging roleplay.   
- **Example:** A broken leg might reduce movement speed until healed, or a head injury could cause temporary spellcasting penalties.   
   
### Scars & Traits   
- **Physical Scars:** Give characters visible scars or disfigurements that tell a story (e.g., a missing finger, a jagged facial scar).   
- **Psychological Traits:** Introduce phobias, nightmares, or personality shifts (e.g., fear of fire, distrust of magic) after near-death experiences.   
 --- 
   
### 2. Fate Points or Luck Mechanics   
- **Fate Points:** Give each player a pool of Fate Points (1-3) to reroll a failed save, avoid a critical hit, or survive a lethal blow once per session. Refresh these points after major milestones or downtime.   
- **Luck as a Resource:** Use the Luck stat as a "get out of death free" card. Players can burn Luck points to survive a fatal blow, but this permanently reduces their Luck score, making future risks even riskier.   
 --- 
   
### 3. "Near-Death" Experiences   
- **Bleeding Out:** Instead of instant death, characters at 0 HP start bleeding out. They can be stabilized by allies (with a skill check or spell) or die after a set number of rounds.   
- **Divine Intervention:** Allow clerics or divine spellcasters to attempt a "last rites" roll to stabilize a dying character, tying survival to the game’s lore.   
 --- 
   
### 4. Consequential Failures   
- **Non-Lethal Traps:** Design traps or hazards that maim, curse, or incapacitate rather than kill. For example:   
    - A poisoned dart might paralyze a character for 1d4 hours instead of killing them.   
    - A pit trap could break a leg instead of impaling the character.   
- **Cursed Items:** Failed rolls with magic items could backfire in creative ways (e.g., a teleport spell sends the character to a random location, or a healing potion turns their skin blue for a week).   
 --- 
   
### 5. "Death" as a Story Beat   
- **Temporary Death:** If a character "dies," their soul is trapped in a liminal space (e.g., the underworld, a ghostly plane). They can return to life by completing a quest or bargaining with a supernatural entity.   
- **Reincarnation:** Allow characters to return as a new race/class, but with a quirk or flaw (e.g., a fighter reincarnated as a goblin with a fear of heights).   
 --- 
   
### 6. Group Morale & Shared Consequences   
- **Morale Checks:** After a near-TPK or major setback, require a group morale check. Failure might mean the party retreats, loses confidence, or gains a temporary penalty.   
- **Reputation Loss:** A failed adventure could tarnish the party’s reputation, making future quests harder or more expensive to secure.   
 --- 
   
### 7. House Rules for Saves & Healing   
- **Graduated Death Saves:** Instead of one failed save = death, require 3 failed saves (like 5e). Each failure inflicts a worsening injury.   
- **Slow Healing:** Reduce healing rates to encourage players to seek out creative solutions (e.g., magical healing, herbalism, or resting in safe havens).   
 --- 
   
### 8. Player Buy-In   
- **Session Zero Discussion:** Talk to your players about the tone they want. Some may love the brutality, while others prefer heroic survival. Adjust the rules to fit the group’s preferences.   
- **Optional Hardcore Mode:** Offer a "hardcore" and "standard" mode for your campaign. Players can choose the level of lethality they’re comfortable with.   
 --- 
   
### Example in Play   
> A fighter is reduced to 0 HP by a troll’s maul. Instead of dying, they roll on the injury table and suffer a shattered kneecap (halved movement speed until healed). The party must carry them to safety, burning resources and time, while avoiding further danger. The fighter now has a limp and a grudge against trolls—adding depth to future encounters.   

 --- 
### Resources for Injury Tables   
- **DCC Community:** Many judges share homebrew injury tables online (e.g., on the [Goodman Games forums](https://goodman-games.com/forums/) or Reddit’s r/DCCRPG).   
- **OSR Inspiration:** Look at games like *Mörk Borg* or *Into the Odd* for creative injury and consequence systems.   
 --- 
   
**Final Thought:**
The goal is to make failure **memorable and meaningful**, not just punishing. Players will feel more invested in their characters’ survival and growth if every close call leaves a mark—literally or figuratively.
