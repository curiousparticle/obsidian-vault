# Speeding up Play   
Keeping players engaged and moving the story forward—without getting bogged down in overplanning—is a common challenge in tabletop RPGs like Dragonbane. Here are some effective strategies to maintain momentum and encourage action:   
 --- 
### 1. Set Clear Expectations and Time Limits   
- **Session Zero**: At the start of the campaign, discuss your preferred playstyle. Emphasize that Dragonbane is designed for fast-paced, decisive action, not endless strategizing.   
- **In-Game Time Pressure**: Introduce urgency—countdowns, approaching enemies, or environmental threats (e.g., a collapsing dungeon, a storm rolling in). Use a visible timer for real-time tension if needed.   
 --- 
   
### 2. Use the Rules to Encourage Action   
- **Initiative and Turn Structure**: Remind players that Dragonbane’s rules require them to act on their turn. They can’t “hold” actions or over-analyze—they must declare and resolve actions promptly.   
- **Consequences for Hesitation**: If players dither, introduce minor setbacks (e.g., enemies gain advantage, traps trigger, NPCs leave). This reinforces that indecision has in-world costs.   
 --- 
   
### 3. Offer Clear, Immediate Choices   
- **Binary or Triad Choices**: Present players with 2-3 clear options (e.g., “Do you charge the bandits, sneak around, or try to negotiate?”). This simplifies decision-making and keeps the story moving.   
- **Avoid Open-Ended Questions**: Instead of “What do you do?”, ask “How do you want to handle this ambush—fight, flee, or trick them?” This focuses their thinking.   
 --- 
   
### 4. Reward Quick, Creative Play   
- **Bonus WP or Advantage**: Reward players who act decisively or creatively with Willpower Points (WP) or narrative advantages. This incentivizes bold choices over perfectionism.   
- **Spotlight Moments**: Highlight and celebrate quick, clever actions. This sets a tone that rewards engagement over overplanning.   
 --- 
   
### 5. Use Scene Framing and Pacing   
- **Start In Media Res**: Begin scenes mid-action (e.g., “The tavern erupts in chaos as the bar fight spills toward you—what do you do?”). This forces immediate engagement.   
- **Cut Scenes Short**: If a scene drags, narrate a summary and move to the next challenge. For example: “After 10 minutes of debate, you finally agree to trust the mysterious stranger. Now, you’re on the road—what’s your next move?”   
 --- 
   
### 6. Embrace “Yes, And…” and “No, But…”   
- **Keep Momentum**: If players propose a plan, say “Yes, and…” or “No, but…” to keep the story flowing. Avoid shutting down ideas outright; instead, redirect or add complications.   
- **Improvise**: If players are stuck, throw in a twist (e.g., “As you debate, the door creaks open—roll for initiative!”).   
 --- 
   
### 7. Prep “Soft” Adventures   
- **Modular Design**: Prepare flexible encounters, NPCs, and locations that can adapt to player choices. This lets you pivot quickly if they go off-script.   
- **Improv Tools**: Use random tables (e.g., for NPC reactions, weather, or complications) to generate on-the-fly content and keep things moving.   
 --- 
   
### 8. Address Analysis Paralysis Directly   
- **Check In**: If a player is stuck, ask, “What’s holding you back? Do you need more info, or should we roll for it?”   
- **Group Consensus**: Encourage quick group votes for major decisions. This prevents one player from stalling the game.   
 --- 
   
### 9. End Sessions with Hooks   
- **Cliffhangers**: Finish each session with a clear hook or unresolved tension (e.g., “The map reveals a hidden door—what’s behind it? Find out next time!”). This gives players something to look forward to and reduces the urge to overplan between sessions.   
 --- 
   
**Example in Play**:   
> Players are debating how to infiltrate a castle.   
> GM: “The guards are changing shifts in 5 minutes. Do you:   

1. Rush the gate now,   
2. Distract them with a diversion, or   
3. Sneak in through the sewers?”
*Players choose option 2.*
**GM**: “Great! Roll Charisma to convince the street performer to help—go!”   
 ---
