# Background Improv   
Creating engaging, structured characters **quickly and within the game**—without overwhelming players with open-ended questions—can be achieved by borrowing techniques from **screenwriting, improv theater, and collaborative storytelling**. Here’s a step-by-step, structured approach tailored for tabletop RPGs like Dragonbane, inspired by methods used by writers and game designers:   
 --- 
### 1. Use "Character Archetype Cards" (Inspired by Screenwriting Tropes)   
**How it works:**
Before or during the first session, hand out (or have players roll for) **3-5 "archetype cards"** that define core aspects of their character. Each card covers a different dimension, limiting choices to spark creativity without paralysis.   
**Example Cards (Mix and Match):**   
- **Role in the Story** (e.g., "The Reluctant Hero," "The Fallen Noble," "The Trickster," "The Guardian")   
- **Flaw/Virtue** (e.g., "Greedy but Loyal," "Brave but Reckless," "Cunning but Distrustful")   
- **Signature Item** (e.g., "A Cursed Dagger," "A Locket with a Loved One’s Portrait," "A Map to a Lost City")   
- **Secret** (e.g., "Wanted for a Crime They Didn’t Commit," "Haunted by a Ghost," "Heir to a Ruined House")   
- **Relationship to the Party** (e.g., "Owes a Debt to Another PC," "Hates One PC’s Faction," "Sworn to Protect a PC")   
   
**Why it works:**   
- **Fast:** Players pick or roll 3-5 cards in minutes.   
- **Structured:** Limits overwhelm by focusing on key traits.   
- **Collaborative:** Encourages ties to other PCs and the world.   
   
**Example:**   
> Player rolls: "The Trickster" (Role), "Cunning but Distrustful" (Flaw/Virtue), "A Map to a Lost City" (Item), "Sworn to Protect a PC" (Relationship).   
> Result: A rogue cartographer who distrusts authority but is bound to protect the party’s cleric—because the cleric’s deity saved their life.   

 --- 
### 2. The "5-Minute Backstory" (Inspired by TV Writers’ Rooms)   
**How it works:**
Ask players to answer **5 rapid-fire questions** in-game, either during character creation or as their character is introduced. Use a **mad-libs-style template** to keep it snappy.   
**Template:**   
1. **"I used to be a [profession], but now I’m [current role] because [inciting incident]."**
*(e.g., "I used to be a temple guard, but now I’m a mercenary because I failed to protect my charge.")*   
2. **"The thing I love most is [person/place/thing], and the thing I fear most is [phobia/enemy]."**   
3. **"I owe [debt] to [NPC/PC], and I’ll never forgive [NPC/PC] for [betrayal]."**   
4. **"My flaw is [trait], and it’s gotten me into trouble when [example]."**   
5. **"My goal in this adventure is [immediate objective], but my dream is [long-term hope]."**   
   
**Why it works:**   
- **Focused:** Each question targets a different layer of depth (motivation, relationships, conflict).   
- **Game-Ready:** Answers create instant hooks for the GM.   
- **Flexible:** Can be done in-character during a tavern scene or campfire.   
   
**Example:**   
> Player: "I used to be a scholar, but now I’m a grave robber because my university sold me out to the cult. The thing I love most is my sister’s locket, and the thing I fear most is being buried alive. I owe my life to Kipper (the halfling rogue), and I’ll never forgive the cult for burning my books. My flaw is curiosity, and it’s gotten me trapped in a tomb before. My goal is to find the Star Gem, but my dream is to rebuild my ruined library."   
> GM’s Hook: The cult is tied to Munafik’s undead priests in the pyramid.   

 --- 
### 3. "The Bonding Scene" (Inspired by Improv Theater)   
**How it works:**
During the first session, run a **short, structured scene** where each player pairs up with another PC for 2-3 minutes. They answer **one prompt** together, then switch partners. Use prompts that force collaboration and reveal secrets.   
**Example Prompts:**   
- *"You both wake up after a battle. What’s the first thing you say to each other?"*   
- *"You find a letter addressed to your partner. Do you read it aloud or hide it?"*   
- *"Your partner is injured. What’s one thing you’d never admit to them while helping?"*   
- *"You’re trapped in a collapsing room. What does your partner do that surprises you?"*   
   
**Why it works:**   
- **Organic:** Builds relationships naturally.   
- **Time-Boxed:** Prevents overthinking.   
- **Revealing:** Uncovers flaws, secrets, and dynamics.   
   
**Example:**   
> Finnian (glutton) and Joruna (foolhardy) are paired.   
> Prompt:"You’re starving in the desert. Finnian finds a single date. What happens?"   
> Finnian:"I offer it to Joruna—then ‘accidentally’ drop it in the sand."   
> Joruna:"I dive for it anyway, then cough up sand for the next hour."   
> Result: Their dynamic is established—Finnian’s greed vs. Joruna’s recklessness.   

 --- 
### 4. "The Three-Object Exercise" (Inspired by Chekhov’s Gun)   
**How it works:**
Each player lists **3 objects their character carries** (one mundane, one valuable, one mysterious). The GM or other players then **ask one question about each object** to flesh it out.   
**Example:**   
| Player   <br> |   Mundane   <br> |           Valuable   <br> |           Mysterious   <br> |
|:--------------|:-----------------|:--------------------------|:----------------------------|
| Kipper   <br> | Lockpicks   <br> | A copper medallion   <br> | A vial of black sand   <br> |
| Joruna   <br> |  A dagger   <br> |           A locket   <br> |           A torn map   <br> |

**Follow-Up Questions:**   
- *Kipper’s vial of black sand:* "Where did you get it?" → *"From a dying Dervish who whispered ‘Skysea’."*   
- *Joruna’s torn map:* "What’s missing?" → *"The location of the Oasis... but I think Finnian has the other half."*   
   
**Why it works:**   
- **Tactile:** Objects ground characters in the world.   
- **Plot Hooks:** The "mysterious" item always ties to the campaign.   
- **Collaborative:** Players riff off each other’s answers.   
 --- 
   
### 5. "The Flaw Challenge" (Inspired by Screenplay Inciting Incidents)   
**How it works:**
At the start of the session, the GM presents each player with a **simple challenge tied to their flaw**. Resolving it (or failing) reveals depth.   
**Examples:**   
- **Kipper (Kleptomaniac):***"You ‘borrow’ a sacred amulet from the temple. The priest demands it back—do you lie, fight, or return it?"*   
- **Joruna (Foolhardy):***"You leap across a chasm without checking the landing. Roll AGL—success means you find a hidden passage; failure means you’re hanging by your fingers."*   
- **Finnian (Gluttonous):***"You eat a ‘magical’ fruit. Roll CON—on a fail, you hallucinate for 1d4 hours (seeing the Efreet’s shadow)."*   
- **Q’wacksum (Greedy):***"You hoard a gem instead of sharing it. Later, a NPC offers you a fortune for it—but it’s cursed."*   
   
**Why it works:**   
- **Immediate Stakes:** Flaws create drama fast.   
- **Player Agency:** Choices define the character.   
- **GM Leverage:** Flaws become recurring themes.   
 --- 
   
### 6. "The One-Sentence Pitch" (Inspired by Elevator Pitches)   
**How it works:**
After creating their character, each player **summarizes them in one sentence** using this formula:   
> "I’m a [role] who [flaw], but I’m trying to [goal] because [motivation]."   

**Examples:**   
- *"I’m a halfling rogue who can’t stop stealing, but I’m trying to break Amun-Re’s curse because my mentor died searching for the Star Gem."*   
- *"I’m a mallard merchant who hoards everything, but I’m here to prove I’m more than my greed—even if it kills me."*   
   
**Why it works:**   
- **Concise:** Forces focus on what matters.   
- **Thematic:** Highlights the character’s arc.   
- **Shareable:** Easy for the GM to remember and reference.   
 --- 
   
### 7. Structured Tie-Ins to the Campaign   
Use a **checklist** to ensure every character is woven into the world:   
- [ ] **Personal Connection to the Quest:***"Why do YOU care about the Star Gems/Efreet?"*
*(e.g., "My family was cursed by Amun-Re," "I owe a debt to Aman Al-Raqib.")*   
- [ ] **Relationship to Another PC:***"Who do you trust/distrust, and why?"*   
- [ ] **Flaw as a Plot Hook:***"How might your flaw complicate the adventure?"*
*(e.g., Greed → tempted by a cursed gem; Kleptomania → steals a key item.)*   
- [ ] **Secret or Lie:***"What’s one thing you haven’t told the party?"*   
**Example for Dragonbane:**   
> Q’wacksum’s greed ties to the Star of Melos-Pelar (a gem he’d sell to the wrong buyer).   
> Joruna’s foolhardiness makes her rush ahead in the pyramid, triggering traps but finding clues.   

 --- 
### 8. Quick-Start Questions for the GM   
If players are stuck, ask **one of these**:   
1. *"What’s the worst thing your character has ever done?"*   
2. *"Who in this room would you die for? Who would you betray?"*   
3. *"What’s a rumor about your character that isn’t true?"*   
4. *"What’s something you’ve lost that you’d kill to get back?"*   
 --- 
   
### Putting It All Together: Example Session Start   
1. **Hand out Archetype Cards** (5 mins).   
2. **5-Minute Backstory** (in-character at the campfire).   
3. **Bonding Scene** (pair up for 2-minute prompts).   
4. **Three-Object Exercise** (share items and answer questions).   
5. **Flaw Challenge** (GM presents a dilemma tied to their flaw).   
6. **One-Sentence Pitch** (summarize their character).   
   
**Result:** In 20-30 minutes, you’ll have **deep, interconnected characters** with clear motivations, flaws, and ties to the world—ready to dive into the adventure.   
 --- 
### Bonus: Cheat Sheet for Players   
Print or share this **one-page guide** for quick character creation:   
```

1. PICK 3 ARCHETYPE CARDS (Role, Flaw, Secret).
2. ANSWER THE 5 BACKSTORY QUESTIONS (in 1-2 sentences each).
3. NAME 3 OBJECTS YOU CARRY (mundane, valuable, mysterious).
4. SHARE YOUR ONE-SENTENCE PITCH.
5. RESPOND TO A FLAW CHALLENGE FROM THE GM.

```
 --- 
### Why This Works for Dragonbane (or Any RPG):   
- **Fast:** No lengthy backstory writing.   
- **Collaborative:** Players build off each other.   
- **Game-Ready:** Flaws and secrets create immediate plot hooks.   
- **Structured:** Limits overwhelm with clear steps.
