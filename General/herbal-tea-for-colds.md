# Herbal tea for colds   
In Switzerland, you have access to some of the best herbal remedies for respiratory issues in the world. Since you are dealing with an irritable, non-productive "itchy" cough and post-nasal drip, you should look for "mucilaginous" herbs (which coat the throat) and "antispasmodic" herbs (which stop the twitching).
Go to your local Apotheke or Drogerie and look for these specific ingredients:   
1. Mallow (Malve / Mauve) — The "Coat"
This is arguably the best herb for that "frog in the throat" feeling. Mallow contains high levels of mucilage, a slippery substance that creates a physical protective layer over your irritated throat lining.   
    - How it feels: It "numbs" the itch by shielding the raw nerves from the air.   
    - Tip: Look for "Käslichrut" tea or pure Mallow blossoms.   
2. Thyme (Thymian / Thym) — The "Relaxant"
Thyme is a powerful antispasmodic. It helps the tiny muscles in your bronchi relax so they don't spasm into a cough.   
    - How it feels: It helps with that "discomfort" you felt when coughing by calming the chest muscles.   
    - The "Swiss Classic": Many Swiss pharmacies sell a "Brust- und Hustentee" (Breast and Cough Tea) mixture that almost always features Thyme as the main ingredient.   
3. Ribwort Plantain (Spitzwegerich / Plantain lancéolé)
This is a "lung-healing" herb often found in Swiss cough syrups and teas. It is both antibacterial and soothing for dry, hacking coughs.   
    - How it feels: It helps reduce the "twitchiness" of the airway.   
4. Icelandic Moss (Isländisch Moos)
This isn't actually a moss, but a lichen. It is famous in Alpine regions for treating "dry" coughs and hoarseness.   
- The "Swiss Fix": If you don't want to drink more tea, look for "Isla-Moos" pastilles. Sucking on these throughout the evening will keep your throat coated much longer than tea will.
How to Prepare Your "Sleep-Well" Tea:   
- Don't use boiling water: Use water that is around 80^\circ\text{C}. Boiling water can sometimes break down the soothing mucilage in Mallow.   
- Steep it long: Let the tea sit for at least 10 minutes to get all the "gooey" soothing properties out.   
- The Honey Rule: Only add the honey once the tea is cool enough to sip comfortably. High heat can damage the beneficial enzymes in the honey.   
- Sip, don't gulp: Take small sips throughout the hour before bed to keep the throat continuously coated.
A Quick "Apotheke" Tip:
If you go to a pharmacy, ask for a "reizlindernder Tee" (irritation-soothing tea). Brands like Sidroga are very common in Switzerland and have specific blends for "Husten- und Reizlinderndstee" that combine several of these herbs.
