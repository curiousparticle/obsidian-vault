# Normal Levels of Loneliness    
It's completely **normal and understandable** to feel anxious and a little lonely when your wife is gone for several weeks. Humans are social creatures, and especially within a close relationship like marriage, your partner becomes a significant source of comfort, companionship, routine, and emotional support.   
Think of it this way:   
- **Disruption of Routine:** Your daily life, habits, and even your physical space are likely intertwined with your wife's presence.Her absence disrupts these routines, which can naturally create a sense of unease or disorganization.   
- **Loss of Companionship:** You likely share meals, conversations, activities, and simply the quiet presence of each other. The absence of this regular companionship naturally leads to feelings of loneliness.   
- **Missing a Core Connection:** Your wife is a primary attachment figure and a source of love, intimacy, and emotional regulation. Her absence means a temporary lack of this vital connection, and it's normal to miss that.   
- **Hormonal/Biological Response:** Some research even suggests that separation from a romantic partner can lead to changes in hormone levels (like oxytocin and cortisol), contributing to feelings of discomfort or stress.   
- **"Absence Makes the Heart Grow Fonder":** This adage exists for a reason! Missing someone you deeply care about is a sign of a strong, healthy bond.   
   
**So, when does it move from normal missing/loneliness to something that might need to be addressed (like Separation Anxiety Disorder)?**   
The key distinction lies in the **intensity, duration, and impact on your daily functioning**.   
Consider these points:   
- **Intensity of Anxiety:**   
    - **Normal:** You feel a sense of worry or unease about her well-being, or about managing things alone, but you can still function.   
    - **Needs Addressing:** The anxiety is overwhelming, leading to panic attacks, constant obsessive worrying about her safety or the relationship, or an inability to focus on anything else. You might feel a desperate urge to constantly check in or experience severe physical symptoms of anxiety.   
- **Loneliness vs. Despair/Helplessness:**   
    - **Normal:** You feel lonely and wish she were there, but you can still find ways to fill your time, enjoy other activities (even if less intensely than with her), and connect with others. You feel capable of managing your life.   
    - **Needs Addressing:** The loneliness is so profound it leads to despair, hopelessness, an inability to motivate yourself, or a feeling of helplessness without her. You might withdraw from other social contacts because they "aren't her."   
- **Impact on Productivity and Sleep:**   
    - **Normal:** You might have some minor difficulty concentrating or sleeping initially, but you adapt over a few days. You can still perform at work and eventually get decent sleep.   
    - **Needs Addressing:** Your anxiety and loneliness significantly impair your ability to work productively (e.g., constant distraction, inability to meet deadlines, frequent errors). Your sleep is severely and consistently disrupted (e.g., unable to fall asleep, frequent awakenings with anxiety, nightmares about separation) for the entire duration she's gone.   
- **Avoidance Behaviors:**   
    - **Normal:** You miss her, but you continue your usual activities, maybe even exploring some new ones.   
    - **Needs Addressing:** You start avoiding situations where you'd be alone (e.g., refusing to stay home alone, constantly seeking out friends even if you don't feel like it, avoiding hobbies you used to enjoy because they feel "empty" without her).   
- **Duration and Resilience:**   
    - **Normal:** The initial few days or the first week might be the hardest, but you gradually adjust and develop coping mechanisms. You look forward to her return, but life doesn't come to a complete halt.   
    - **Needs Addressing:** The intense anxiety and loneliness persist throughout the entire several weeks, with little to no improvement, and you feel entirely "stuck."   
   
**When to Consider Addressing It (and How):**   
If your experience leans more towards the "Needs Addressing" side, it would be beneficial to explore it. This doesn't necessarily mean there's something "wrong" with you, but rather that you could develop more robust coping mechanisms and internal resources.   
This is exactly where the David Burns "Feeling Good" principles and CBT come in. The goal wouldn't be to make you *not* miss your wife (that's unrealistic and unhealthy), but rather to help you:   
1. **Manage the anxious thoughts:** Challenge catastrophic predictions about her safety or your own ability to cope.   
2. **Cope with the loneliness:** Develop activities and social connections that can sustain you and provide enjoyment during her absence.   
3. **Build self-efficacy:** Strengthen your belief in your own ability to function and find happiness independently.   
4. **Improve sleep:** Apply CBT-I principles to address sleep disturbances exacerbated by her absence.   
   
**In conclusion:** Acknowledge that it's okay to feel some anxiety and loneliness. It's a sign of a deep connection. However, if these feelings become debilitating, consistently interfere with your work or sleep, or lead to significant distress or avoidance, then it's a good idea to consider professional help (like a therapist specializing in CBT) to develop healthier coping strategies.
