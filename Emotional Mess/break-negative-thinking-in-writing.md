# Break Negative Thinking in Writing   
Hey A,   
It sounds like you’ve got a classic tug‑of‑war going on between your goals and the pull of distractions. That’s something many of us wrestle with, so you’re definitely not alone. Below are a few practical ideas you can try out—feel free to mix, match, or adapt them to fit your style.   
 --- 
### 1. Spot the Thought Loop   
**What’s happening?**   
When a negative thought pops up (“I’m not good enough,” “I’ll never finish this”), it often triggers a quick escape (e.g., opening a game).   
**How to interrupt it:**   
1. **Name it** – Silently say, “I’m having a self‑critical thought.”   
2. **Pause** – Take a brief 3‑second breath before acting.   
3. **Reframe** – Replace the thought with a neutral statement: “I’m feeling stuck right now; I can take a small step.”   
   
Practicing this “name‑pause‑reframe” loop even for a few minutes each day can gradually weaken the automatic trigger.   
--   
## 2. Tiny‑Step Start‑Up   
Large tasks feel intimidating, which fuels the negative narrative. Break anything you need to do into the *smallest* possible first step—something you could start in under two minutes.   
- **Example:** Instead of “write the report,” start with “open the document and type the title.”   
- Once you’ve taken that micro‑action, momentum often carries you forward, and the brain rewards you with a sense of progress.   
 --- 
   
### 3. Structured Work Blocks (Pomodoro‑Lite)   
A light version of the Pomodoro technique works well if you tend to drift into games:   
|             Cycle   <br> |                                                                                                                                            Action   <br> |
|:-------------------------|:---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
|         **Focus**   <br> |                                      Set a timer for **12–15 minutes**. During this window, commit to a single task and keep the game tab closed.   <br> |
|   **Micro‑Break**   <br> |       When the timer rings, allow yourself a **2‑minute** break—stretch, grab a drink, or even glance at a game screenshot (but don’t launch it).   <br> |
|        **Repeat**   <br> |                            Do 3–4 cycles, then treat yourself to a longer **10‑minute** break where you can play a short game session guilt‑free.   <br> |

The key is that the *reward* (gaming) comes **after** a concrete work chunk, not before.   
 --- 
### 4. Environment Tweaks   
- **App blockers** – Use extensions like *StayFocusd* (Chrome) or *Freedom* (desktop) to limit gaming sites during work hours.   
- **Physical cue** – Keep a sticky note on your monitor that says something like “One step forward” or “Start small.” Visual reminders can nudge you back when you notice the urge to drift.   
- **Dedicated workspace** – If possible, separate where you work from where you game (different room, different desk). The brain starts associating each space with its primary activity.   
 --- 
   
### 5. Mindful Reset Ritual   
When you catch yourself spiraling into negativity, try a quick reset ritual:   
1. **5‑second grounding** – Feel your feet on the floor, notice three things you can see, hear, and touch.   
2. **One‑sentence gratitude** – Write or think of a single thing you’re grateful for right now.   
3. **Set a micro‑goal** – “I will spend the next 5 minutes outlining the first paragraph.”   
   
## Even a brief pause can shift the mental state from rumination to purposeful action.   
### 6. Track Progress (and Celebrate)   
Create a simple log—paper notebook, spreadsheet, or a notes app—where you record:   
- Date & time of each work block   
- What you accomplished (even if it’s just “opened the document”)   
- How you felt afterward (e.g., “more focused,” “still distracted”)   
   
## Seeing a streak of completed blocks builds confidence and provides data to spot patterns (e.g., certain times of day when you’re most prone to gaming).   
### 7. Compassion Over Criticism   
Finally, be gentle with yourself. Negative self‑talk often amplifies the very behavior you want to change. When you notice a harsh iner voice, try responding as you would to a friend: “It’s okay to feel stuck; let’s try a tiny step together.” Over time, this compassionate inner dialogue can replace the critical one.
