# Reframing to Reduce Anxiety    
Thank you for sharing all of this. It's incredibly difficult to be in the situation you're in, and your feelings of anger, confusion, and anxiety are completely valid. It sounds like you're caught in a painful state of limbo where you're receiving mixed signals. She says she loves you and has hope, but her actions—asking for space, being unclear about what that means, and a lack of progress—are telling a different story.   
It's a very human reaction to feel angry when you're being hurt, especially when the source of that hurt is someone you love. The anger you're feeling is likely a protective response, a way of guarding yourself against further pain and rejection. And the impulse to avoid her is a natural consequence of that.   
It's clear you're not getting what you need, which is a sense of certainty and a clear path forward. Without that, it's impossible for your anxiety to go down.   
Here are a few ways to reframe the situation for yourself that may help you manage the anger and anxiety:   
## Reframe Your Role from "Fixer" to "Observer"   
You've likely been trying to fix things, to make her feel secure, and to find a way to get back to a good place. It's time to step back from that role. You can't fix her feelings or make her decide what she wants.   
Instead, try to shift your mindset to that of an observer of the situation. Your job is no longer to solve her problems, but to observe what's happening and react to it with a focus on your own well-being.   
- **Actionable step:** Focus on understanding, not solving. For example, when she says she wants "space," instead of getting angry, try to observe that this is her way of coping. When she shows you physical affection, observe that this is likely a sign of her conflicting feelings. You can accept these things without taking them on as your responsibility to fix.   
 --- 
   
## Acknowledge the Mixed Signals as the Root of Your Pain   
Your anger and anxiety are not irrational; they are a direct and logical response to the mixed signals you're receiving. It's confusing to be told "I love you" while also being asked for "space." This creates a state of cognitive dissonance, where two conflicting ideas are held at the same time, which is incredibly stressful for the human brain.   
- **Actionable step:** Give yourself permission to be angry and anxious about the mixed messages. Tell yourself, "It makes sense that I'm angry and anxious. Anyone would be in this situation. My feelings are a direct result of being in limbo." This validation can reduce the shame you might feel about your anger and help you process it more healthily.   
 --- 
   
## **Shift Your Focus from "Us" to "Me"**   
Right now, you're deeply focused on the health of the relationship and her emotional state. You're trying to figure out what she wants. The problem is that she can't give you a clear answer, and you can't wait for her forever.   
It’s time to pivot and focus on your own needs and what you can control.   
- **Actionable step:** Begin to make decisions for yourself, separate from the status of your marriage. What do you need right now? More time with friends? A new hobby? A clearer separation agreement? Start taking small steps to create a life for yourself that isn't entirely dependent on her emotional journey. This doesn't mean you're giving up on the marriage; it means you're no longer putting your own life on hold while you wait for her to figure things out.   
 --- 
   
You are in a painful and difficult situation, and it's okay to feel overwhelmed. Your anger and anxiety are signposts telling you that your needs for clarity and certainty are not being met. By reframing your role, acknowledging the cause of your pain, and shifting your focus to yourself, you may be able to reduce your emotional turmoil and find a path toward stability, no matter what she decides.
