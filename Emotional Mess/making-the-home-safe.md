# Making the home safe   
That makes perfect sense, and you've already demonstrated remarkable insight into your coping mechanisms.   
The fact that walking, talking to others, and building things (like cabinets and Legos) work, while staying home is stressful, points to the following:   
### 1. Why Your Current Coping Works   
- **Walking:** This is a fantastic coping mechanism. It's a form of **Intense Exercise** (a DBT distress tolerance skill), which physically changes your body chemistry and adrenaline levels. The rhythmic, forward motion is naturally soothing and therapeutic.   
- **Talking to Yourself/Others:** This is **Verbal Processing** and **Reality Checking**. It allows you to externalize the panic and challenge the irrational fear ("I am abandoned") with rational facts ("My friend is just at work, and I am safe").   
- **Building/Legos:** These are **Mastery** and **Distraction** activities. They force your attention onto a task that requires concentration, preventing catastrophic thoughts from taking over, and the completion of a project reinforces your sense of self-competence and independence.   
   
### 2. Why Home is Stressful   
Your home, when empty, is a **triggering environment**—a place of perceived **absence and vulnerability**. In the context of a fear of abandonment, home equals:   
- **Lack of Connection:** It's a physical reminder that no one else is present to provide security or validation.   
- **Emotional Stillness:** The quiet and stillness leave you exposed to your *internal* emotional landscape, which is where the pain of abandonment lives.   
- **The "Unsolvable" Problem:** The anxiety is rooted in a childhood feeling that home *wasn't safe* or caregivers *weren't consistently present*.   
   
The challenge is to re-wire your brain to see your home not as a place of absence, but as a **safe base for your own presence.**

## Strategies to Reclaim Your Home and Expand Your Coping Toolkit   
The goal is to move from **avoidance** (leaving the house) to **tolerance** and **mastery** (being at home and calm).   
### 1. The Home Re-Wiring Strategy (Desensitization)   
You need to create a new emotional association with your home through small, repeated, positive exposures.   
|             **Phase**   <br> |                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                 **Strategy**   <br> |                                                                                                                **Purpose**   <br> |
|:-----------------------------|:------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|:----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
|   **Micro-Exposures**   <br> |                                                                                          Set a timer for just **15 minutes** of structured alone time at home. Use a fun, absorbing activity (e.g., a puzzle, a video game, or a compelling book). When the timer goes off, you are *allowed* to leave or call someone. Gradually increase this time by 5 minutes each week.   <br> |                                  Builds **Distress Tolerance** and proves to your brain that the time limit is manageable.   <br> |
| **Sensory Anchoring**   <br> | Engage your senses in the room to make it feel safe and *full*. **Smell:** Light a specific, calming candle *only*when you are alone. **Sound:** Play familiar, comforting sounds (e.g., a favorite podcast, a specific playlist, ambient background noise like a fan or a coffee shop soundtrack). **Touch:** Create a "safe corner" with your softest blanket and pillows.   <br> |             Uses **Self-Soothing** to intentionally link a peaceful, regulated state with the physical space of your home.   <br> |
| **"I'm Here" Ritual**   <br> |                                                                                                     When you enter your empty home, perform a small ritual to affirm your own presence. This could be opening all the blinds, turning on all the lights, or making a cup of tea. As you do it, say (silently or aloud): **"I am home. I am safe. I will not leave myself."**   <br> | Reinforces **Self-Reliance** and turns the act of being alone from a passive state into an **active, intentional choice.**   <br> |

### 2. Expanding Your Active Coping (DBT Skills at Home)   
Since you like action-oriented coping (walking, building), here are more "doing" skills to use when the anxiety starts:   
|                             **Anxiety Level**   <br> |                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                             **Recommended Strategy**   <br> |                                                                                             **Purpose**   <br> |
|:-----------------------------------------------------|:----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|:---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **High Spike / Crisis** (Racing heart, panic)   <br> |                                                                                                          **Intense, Short-Burst Activity (TIPP Skill):** Do **30 seconds of intense movement** (e.g., wall sits, push-ups, running in place). This quickly burns off the adrenaline spike that is fueling the panic.   <br> |                                                                   Physically resets the nervous system.   <br> |
|         **Medium Anxiety**(Worrying thoughts)   <br> | **Productive Distraction (ACCEPTS Skill):** Engage in detailed, high-focus tasks that leave little room for catastrophic thinking. **Examples:** **Jigsaw Puzzles** (similar to Legos but static), **Complex Cooking/Baking** (focus on measurements and steps), or **Organizing** a small area (a drawer, a shelf).   <br> | Builds **Mastery** and redirects the pre-frontal cortex (the thinking brain) onto a non-emotional task.   <br> |
|           **Low-Level Anxiety**(Restlessness)   <br> |                                                                                  **Mindful Movement (Walking Alternative):** Do a full-body **Stretch or Yin Yoga Routine** in your living room. The slow, intentional stretching forces you to connect with your body and breath, which is the core of mindfulness.   <br> |   Uses movement to **anchor** you in the present, just like walking, but within the walls of your home.   <br> |

By practicing these steps, you gradually transform the "home alone" feeling from a threat of abandonment into an opportunity for **self-compassion, competence, and self-connection.**
