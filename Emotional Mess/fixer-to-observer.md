# Fixer to Observer   
Switching from a "fixer" to an "observer" is a powerful shift that can help you reclaim your emotional energy and reduce your anxiety. It's about changing your internal focus from what you **can do** for her to what you **can do** for yourself.   
Here's how to think about this new role and make the change.   
### The Fixer's Mindset vs. The Observer's Mindset   
The **fixer's mindset** is all about problem-solving. When you're in this mode, your brain is constantly working to:   
- **Identify problems:** *What is she unhappy about? How can I make her feel better?*   
- **Propose solutions:** *Maybe if I'm more affectionate, she'll feel more secure. Maybe if we take a vacation, things will get better.*   
- **Take on responsibility:** *Her unhappiness is my fault. It's my job to fix this.*   
   
This mindset keeps you on a hamster wheel of trying, failing, and feeling rejected, which fuels your anger and anxiety.   
The **observer's mindset**, on the other hand, is about understanding, not controlling. When you're in this mode, your brain is focused on:   
- **Gathering information:** *She said she wants space. I'll just notice what that looks like and what it feels like to me.*   
- **Accepting reality:** *This is the situation we're in right now. She is feeling insecure, and I am feeling confused and angry.*   
- **Focusing on your own reactions:** *I'm feeling angry right now. Why is that? What do I need to do to take care of myself?*   
   
This mindset gives you a sense of agency and stability because it grounds you in what's real and what you can actually control: your own thoughts, feelings, and actions.   
### How to Practice Being an Observer   
This isn't an easy switch to flip, but you can build this muscle with practice.   
1. **Notice the Fixer Impulse.** The next time your wife says something that triggers you—like her being unclear about what "space" means—pay attention to your first instinct. Does your mind immediately start racing for a solution? That's your fixer impulse. Acknowledge it without judgment: *"There's my fixer instinct again."*   
2. **Practice Active Listening Without a Solution.** When your wife talks about her feelings, just listen. Your only job is to hear what she's saying. Don't immediately jump to a solution or a counter-argument. You could respond with something as simple as, "Thank you for sharing that with me," or "I hear what you're saying." This signals that you're there to listen, not to fix.   
3. **Validate Your Own Feelings.** When you feel the familiar pang of rejection or anger, don't ignore it. Instead of turning it against her, turn it inward with compassion. Say to yourself, *"I'm feeling angry right now because I'm being given mixed signals, and that's incredibly confusing. It's okay for me to feel this way."* This simple act of self-validation can help dissipate the intensity of the emotion.   
4. **Create Your Own Path Forward.** An observer doesn't wait for the other person to make a move. An observer decides what is best for their own well-being.   
    - What kind of "space" do **you** need?   
    - What are **your** boundaries around communication and affection during this time?   
    - What do **you** need to feel respected and safe in this difficult situation?   
   
By focusing on these questions, you are taking back control of your own emotional state. You're no longer a passenger on her emotional journey; you're the captain of your own ship, navigating a difficult storm.   
This shift is not a sign of giving up on the relationship; it's a sign of a healthy focus on your own well-being. It allows you to be a more stable presence, which, ironically, might be more helpful to her than any "fixing" you could attempt.
